The Short Story that Inspired the 2024 Met Gala’s Theme

In an unnamed place and time, a majestic villa overlooks a garden of delicate, glass-stemmed flowers. Home to Count Axel and the Countess, the otherwise elegant landscape is unsettled by a dark blot on the horizon: an army of furious workers, drawing nearer day by day. To slow the army’s approach, Axel plucks the time flowers from his garden; each one dissolves in a burst of light which rewinds time itself, and so the threatening horde shifts back on the skyline. This solution, however, is only temporary. The garden of time is faltering, the flowers are almost all gone, and the masses will soon arrive to take their long-awaited revenge.

Review: Talking to Strangers

Malcolm Gladwell’s book Talking to Strangers examines the ways we misinterpret and fail to communicate with each other. Gladwell presents readers with compelling case studies and fascinating research that show just how bad humans are at spotting deception, even at close quarters, and how wrong we are to think we are good judges of character.

Movie vs. Book: Little Women

Little Women has been adapted so many times over the years that certain images have become cemented in the public imagination: the family gathered around a letter from Father at Christmastime, the burnt manuscript, Amy falling through the ice, Jo cutting her hair to pay for Marmee’s journey. Greta Gerwig’s new movie contains all of these requisite scenes, but it also reimagines the story by turning it into a work of art about its own making, further blurring the lines between author Louisa May Alcott and her semi-autobiographical heroine, Jo March.

Stop Calling Romeo and Juliet a Love Story

Romeo and Juliet is perhaps the most well-known love story in the English language; it is so famous that the titular characters have ascended to the status of metaphor. The play is seen as the archetypal romantic tragedy, the quintessential tragic love story. However, Romeo and Juliet isn’t a love story, it’s simply tragic. The couple’s “love,” presented with ample doses of irony and bad taste, is merely a caricature. Furthermore, lest readers forget, Romeo and Juliet are teenagers — Juliet on the cusp of turning 14, Romeo likely just a few years older than her.

A Timeless Message from The Veldt

As our society becomes increasingly digitized, The Veldt feels more relevant than ever, despite being written by Ray Bradbury more than 70 years ago. In Bradbury’s short story, the Hadley family lives a life of leisure in a fully automated house. The “Happylife Home” does it all: it cooks for the Hadleys, helps them bathe, and even ties their shoelaces. However, parents George and Lydia become increasingly concerned by their children Peter and Wendy's fascination with the “nursery,” a virtual reality room that can realistically reproduce any place they imagine.

Must-Read Classics

The classics aren't just “old books.” They’re thought-provoking, time-transcending, cultural artifacts of superb quality. Reading the classics allows us to gain a deeper understanding of the human experience, exposes us to timeless themes and universal messages, and encourages personal growth and development, which in turn equips us with the skills and knowledge to navigate life's complexities.

Review: Small Great Things

Jodi Picoult's novel Small Great Things follows the story of Ruth, an experienced nurse who also happens to be the only African-American at a small hospital in New Haven. One day, when Ruth tries to examine a newborn, she unexpectedly faces resistance, as the father turns out to be a white supremacist. Then, the baby goes into cardiac arrest. Ruth tries and fails to resuscitate the baby, the father accuses Ruth of murder, and everything begins to fall apart.

Explaining Animal Farm's Ending

“The creatures outside looked from pig to man, and from man to pig, and from pig to man again; but already it was impossible to say which was which.” The last line of George Orwell's Animal Farm is impactful, to say the least, but some readers may find themselves confused by the allegorical nature of the story.

Crying in Hmart is Overrated

Michelle Zauner’s memoir Crying in Hmart is, among other things, about Korean food, the confusion that comes with a biracial identity, and Zauner’s fraught relationship with her hyper-demanding Korean mother, who eventually died to cancer. Shortly after Crying in Hmart’s release, it became wildly successful — lauded as “an artistic triumph,” the book won the 2021 Goodreads Choice Award for Memoir & Autobiography, stayed on the New York Times best-seller list for more than 40 weeks, and may even be adapted to the big screen. Yet, readers with reservations about Zauner’s memoir may be relieved to discover that there are others who share their opinions. At times, Crying in Hmart is incoherent, unengaging, and excessively flowery.
